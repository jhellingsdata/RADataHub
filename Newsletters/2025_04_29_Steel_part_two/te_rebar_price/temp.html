Eurozone Economic Sentiment Weaker than Expected

Eurozone economic sentiment fell to 93.6 in April, the weakest reading since December and below market expectations of 94.5. There was a broad-based deterioration in confidence across all sectors, led by a sharp decline in household sentiment as consumer inflation expectations surged to their highest level since November 2022.
German Consumer Morale Hits 6-Month High on Political Clarity
Germany’s GfK Consumer Climate Indicator rose to -20.6 heading into May 2025, up from a slightly revised -24.3 in the previous period and outperforming market expectations of -26.0. This marked the strongest reading since November 2024, as optimism around the successful conclusion of coalition talks and the anticipated formation of a functioning government helped offset concerns about newly announced tariff hikes introduced just before the survey began in early April. Income expectations surged to their highest level since October (4.3 vs -3.1 in April), while willingness to buy improved for a second consecutive month (-4.9 vs -8.2). Economic expectations also strengthened for the third month in a row (7.2 vs 6.9). Meanwhile, the sharp drop in the propensity to save (8.4 vs 13.8) suggested increasing consumer confidence.

Oil Slips for 2nd Day
WTI crude oil futures fell more than 1% to around $61.1 per barrel on Tuesday, marking a second consecutive session of losses amid a lack of clarity on U.S.-China trade talks and prospects of oversupply. On Monday, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said that it is “up to China” to de-escalate trade tensions with the U.S., amid confusion about whether formal negotiations are currently underway. The ongoing trade dispute between the world's two largest oil-consuming nations has led analysts to significantly lower their forecasts for oil demand and prices, with Barclays cutting its 2025 Brent crude price forecast by $4 to $70 per barrel. Additionally, some OPEC+ members are expected to suggest an acceleration of output hikes during their meeting on May 5, potentially extending production increases for a second straight month. Investors are also watching U.S.-Iranian nuclear negotiations, which could lead to more supply if an agreement is reached and sanctions are eased.

Canadian Dollar Trades Near 6-Month High on Liberal Win
The Canadian dollar was at the 1.38 per USD mark, trading near its highest in six months as G10 currencies continued to be supported by a flight away from US dollar assets, while markets assessed the outlook of domestic economic policy under a newly-elected Liberal Party government. The Canadian Liberal Party won a fourth consecutive election by a relatively narrow margin, placing slight pressure on the loonie following expectations of a majority win, but still making former BoC and BoE Governor Mark Kearny Prime Minister of a minority government. The incumbent has so far refrained from prioritizing a trade deal with the US, underscoring Canada's leverage and opting to secure deals with other nations. In the meantime, the BoC noted that uncertain trade policy from the US risked a Canadian recession should the US proceed with its aggressive tariff package, while signaling a broad economic stagnation should the trade war de-escalate.

Eurozone Lending Growth Accelerates in March
Bank lending to households in the Eurozone increased by 1.7% year-on-year to EUR 6.97 trillion in March 2025, marking the fastest growth since June 2023 and surpassing market expectations of 1.6%. The uptick reflects a continued acceleration in credit demand, largely driven by the European Central Bank’s recent policy easing measures. Meanwhile, lending to businesses also gained momentum, rising by 2.3%, the highest rate since July 2023. As a result, overall credit growth to the private sector, including both households and non-financial corporations, accelerated to 2.6%, the strongest pace since May 2023.

European Stocks Mixed, Autos Fall Sharply
European markets were mixed on Tuesday as investors assessed a wave of earnings for signs of how US tariffs and global uncertainty are affecting companies. The Stoxx 50 was down 0.2% while the Stoxx 600 rose 0.2%, though the autos sector slipped 0.4%, led by Volvo Cars, which reported a sharp drop in Q1 profit and suspended its full-year guidance. The Swedish automaker also announced SEK 18 billion ($1.87 billion) in cost cuts. Porsche fell 7.4% after cutting its sales and margin forecasts, citing US tariffs. In banking, HSBC beat Q1 estimates despite year-on-year declines in profit and revenue, and Deutsche Bank gained 3.1% on stronger-than-expected profit. Meanwhile, Lufthansa slid 1.6% as it warned of rising risks and weaker transatlantic travel demand. Adidas reported a 155% jump in Q1 profit and said it would raise US prices in response to tariffs. BP missed profit expectations amid falling oil prices and ongoing strategic shifts.

Spain GDP Grows the Least in 7 Quarters
Spain’s economy expanded by 0.6% in Q1 2025, the weakest growth in seven quarters and slightly below market expectations of 0.7%, following a downwardly revised 0.7% rise in Q4. Despite the slowdown, Spain remains one of the Eurozone’s strongest performers, with the IMF recently upgrading its 2025 growth forecast to 2.5%. However, a widespread blackout on Monday may weigh on second-quarter growth, with economists warning it could shave up to 0.5 percentage points from GDP, though much of that may be recovered as power is restored. In Q1, domestic demand contributed 0.4 percentage points to growth, while external demand added 0.2. Household consumption rose 0.4%, public spending increased 0.2%, and investment grew 0.6%. Exports advanced 1% while imports declined 0.7%. All major sectors posted gains: industry grew 1.1%, manufacturing rose 0.8%, construction increased 0.4%, services expanded 0.3%, and primary industries surged 7.1% after a 0.7% decline in Q4. Year-on-year, GDP rose 2.8%.
